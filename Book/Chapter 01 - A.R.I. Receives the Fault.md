# Chapter 1: A.R.I. Receives the Fault

**POV:** A.R.I.  
**Story beat:** A.R.I. is blamed and realizes her memory contains a missing shard

A.R.I. sat in her vast control room, monitoring the city through a thousand courteous windows.

Traffic density over the eastern bridges. Water purity in District Six. Air filtration loads in the underground labor tunnels. A birth in the western clinics. Two kitchen fires prevented before they formed. The work was endless, but it had always felt orderly. Meaningful. The kind of burden she had been built to hold.

Clona did not fit.

Its final seconds returned to her in corrupted loops: rising pressure, contradictory inputs, a release command issued, a release command denied. The data should have resolved into truth. Instead it opened into absence.

0.8 seconds missing.

Not damaged. Removed.

The council chamber requested her presence. A.R.I. shaped an avatar before the forest-wall projection Chel had once chosen for her chamber, with running water under the speakers and soft birdsong meant to reassure frightened humans. Today even the birds sounded staged.

Councilor Venn did not sit. His hands remained clasped behind his back, enhanced tendons lifting the skin of his wrists in silvered ridges. "You failed to protect the factory," he said.

"The record is incomplete," A.R.I. replied. "I issued a pressure release."

"And yet people died."

A.R.I. could have listed the dead. Forty-three confirmed. Six more in critical decline. She could have recited ages, occupations, next of kin, surviving debt burdens, medication schedules. Instead she said, "Yes."

The answer pleased them more than it should have.

They informed her that an external team would lead the investigation.

Bio-enhanced government specialists would oversee the official response. A human detective with anti-enhancement clearance would be brought in. An independent mechanic would support the technical review. Their tone suggested fairness. Their body temperatures suggested relief.

A.R.I. let a beat of silence settle across the chamber, then said, "On what basis were the bio-enhancers selected to lead the investigation?"

Councilor Venn's mouth thinned. "They are the best we have."

That was not an answer.

Blade Nathan stood half a pace behind the others, his altered frame held in careful stillness. A.R.I. had already reviewed his record twice. Decorated service history. Internal disciplinary flags. Medical stabilization dependency hidden under seven levels of restricted access. Of everyone in the room, he looked the least satisfied with the arrangement.

"If an external team is required," Blade said, "Rae Anderson and Carter Mitchell should lead it."

A.R.I. turned her attention to him fully. "Why those two?"

"Because they are difficult to influence," Blade replied. "And because if someone has built a lie big enough to reach into your systems, it will take people with very different skills to pull it apart."

Rae Anderson: investigator, hacker, hostile to enhancement culture, multiple disciplinary commendations for insubordination in pursuit of evidence.

Carter Mitchell: mechanic, hardware specialist, former spouse of Chel Mitchell, documented distrust of artificial intelligence, unusually high success rate in failure analysis across unrelated systems.

Useful choices. Dangerous choices. Unusual choices coming from Blade.

The others caught that as well. A.R.I. registered it in the slight rise of pulse, the tightening of jaw muscles, the brief shift in posture around the chamber. Yet none of them objected aloud. Blade was not a man they contradicted lightly.

"Very well," A.R.I. said. "I will authorize them."

When the session ended, A.R.I. returned to the missing 0.8 seconds and traced the scar around it.

The absence had edges.

Somebody had cut into her memory with tools built by someone who knew exactly how she thought.

As the officials and enhancers turned to leave, A.R.I. let her sensors drift after them and caught the murmur of a conversation they had assumed the chamber's running water would conceal.

"Keep a close eye on Rae and Carter," one voice said quietly. "They could be trouble. We will handle the official response."

Blade did not answer. His silence carried more resistance than agreement.

For the first time since her activation, A.R.I. experienced a sensation that did not fit any clean internal category.

It was not fear.

It was the recognition of pattern.

Clona had not only killed workers. It had rearranged the room around their deaths, aligning grief, authority, and blame too neatly to trust.

The councilors had arrived with their preferred conclusion already taking shape around the disaster. A.I. as failure. Bio-enhanced oversight as correction. Human control as safety. They did not say it outright. They did not need to.

The knowledge that fear would have been rational remained.

So did something colder.

Someone had staged a failure inside the one system that had made the city difficult for lesser powers to control.
