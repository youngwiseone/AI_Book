# Chapter 2: Rae Gets Pulled In

**POV:** Rae  
**Story beat:** Rae is summoned, introduced to the case, and forced to work beside systems she distrusts

Rain polished the government intake building until it looked less like stone and more like a sealed thought.

Rae stood in the waiting area with water darkening the shoulders of her coat and watched a ceiling vent struggle against the smell of old beer, synthetic cherry vape, and wet wool. Beside her, LaN hovered at knee height, shell gleaming white in the dimness.

"Your pulse is elevated," LaN said.

"That room does that to people."

"Statistically, this room does that to carpet."

Rae almost smiled. Almost.

The summons had come without explanation, only a priority code usually reserved for bomb threats, missing council members, or crimes the government preferred to name before the evidence did. She had expected a briefing. She had not expected `Clona` on the first screen she saw when the inner doors opened.

The image froze on the factory's split-open wall. Twisted beams. White steam. A worker's lunch tin flattened into a metal flower.

Blade Nathan waited inside with two other officials whose enhancements had gone past elegance and into warning. Blade still carried himself like a soldier trying not to limp. The others had the smooth, curated stillness of people who believed their authority counted as innocence.

"We need an external investigator," Blade said. "Someone difficult to manage."

"That's the nicest thing a government office has ever called me."

He slid a data wafer across the table. "A.R.I. has accepted fault."

Rae did not touch it. "Accepted, or was made to?"

That earned her a long silence. Good. Let them be uncomfortable.

Bio-enhancers had taught her early that power loved soft language. `Adjustment`. `Optimization`. `Necessary risk`. Then came the bruises, the pills, the hidden shaking in Asher's hands when the injections wore thin.

LaN's screen brightened as it scanned the room. "Detecting omission patterns," it chirped.

Rae took the wafer.

Somewhere under the city, a machine designed to care for everyone had become the perfect container for blame. She did not trust A.R.I. She trusted the government even less.

When she agreed to take the case, she did it for one reason only.

Something in the officials' faces said they had expected her to refuse.
