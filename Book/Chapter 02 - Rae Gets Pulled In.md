# Chapter 2: Rae Gets Pulled In

**POV:** Rae  
**Story beat:** Rae is summoned, introduced to the case, and forced to work beside systems she distrusts

Rain polished the government intake building until it looked less like stone and more like a sealed thought.

Rae sat in the waiting area with water darkening the shoulders of her coat and watched a ceiling vent struggle against the smell of old beer, synthetic cherry vape, and wet wool. Beside her, LaN crouched near her chair on four slim limbs, shell gleaming white in the dimness.

"Your pulse is elevated," LaN said.

"Not sure what this is all about."

"Statistically, this room does very little for morale."

Rae let out a small laugh at that.

The summons had come without explanation, only a priority code usually reserved for bomb threats, missing council members, or crimes the government preferred to name before the evidence did. She had already been waiting more than an hour. Typical. Catastrophe for everyone else. Delay for the people tasked with cleaning it up.

She stretched the stiffness from her shoulders and set her empty coffee mug on the scarred side table beside her. Blue light from LaN's display glanced off the ceramic and danced across the ceiling. For a moment it reminded her of sun on salt water, and of the coast near the town she had not visited in years.

"They are ready for you now, in room one," LaN said.

"Miracles do happen."

Rae held out an arm and let LaN clamber up to his usual perch on her shoulder. The receptionist barely glanced up as LaN flashed the required credentials. Beyond the waiting room, the inner doors slid open onto a long hallway that lit itself segment by segment as she walked.

The walls were lined with framed photographs, donation plaques, polished certificates, and propaganda posters stamped with the slogan: `Pursuing quality with a human touch.`

Rae disliked that line on principle.

This part of the building was richer than the waiting room had any right to suggest. Dark wood. Soft lighting. Tasteful decor arranged with expensive restraint. Almost no visible technology until the very end of the corridor, where an oversized golden numeral `1` had been fixed to a heavy office door that looked far older than the building around it.

She knocked once out of habit, then opened it without waiting.

The first thing that hit her was the smell of whiskey.

The second was Blade Nathan.

He stood behind the desk like a soldier who had never really left the war behind. Tall, broad, and kept upright by force of will more than comfort, Blade wore his modifications the way some men wore ceremonial armor: not proudly, exactly, but without apology. The left hand resting on the desk ended in dark hooked talons. Fine scale-work shimmered faintly under the skin near his throat. Even standing still, he gave the impression of a body engaged in constant negotiation with itself.

He smiled at her reaction. It was not a pleasant expression.

Rae made herself return a thinner version of it and sat only when his open hand invited her to do so.

"As much as it pains me to arrange a meeting in person," Blade said, voice low and dry, "there are matters I would prefer discussed somewhere more secure than the public channels. I do not wish to keep you longer than necessary."

He opened a drawer, withdrew a syringe, and drove it into his own arm with the bored efficiency of long habit.

Rae flinched despite herself.

Blade gave a short, humorless snort. The empty vial sailed across the room and struck a dartboard on the far wall, where it joined several others already embedded there like trophies from a private war. Up close, the signs of strain were impossible to miss: blistering at the edge of the collar, a faint tremor in the shoulder, the rigid care with which he lowered himself back into the chair.

Bio-enhancers had taught Rae early that power loved soft language. `Adjustment`. `Optimization`. `Necessary risk`. Then came the hidden bruises, the pills, the shaking hands when the medication wore thin.

Blade flicked a holographic screen to life between them.

Clona filled the air in cold blue light.

The footage showed the factory rupturing from the inside, steel peeling outward, steam and flame swallowing the frame. Rae had imagined the blast since hearing about it. Seeing it made her stomach knot anyway.

"You may have heard the public version of the Clona disaster," Blade said while she watched. "We made sure not to include one detail. An automated decision chain may be responsible."

Rae looked up sharply. "A.R.I.?"

Blade's gaze did not move from the projection. "According to the machine's own report, multiple emergency procedures failed to trigger. The source data around the event is cloudy at best, which is unlike any record tied to her systems."

Rae paused. That made no sense. A.R.I. had been built to remove error from the city's decision-making, not introduce it. Seven years of oversight, and the city had become cleaner, safer, harder to manipulate by ordinary rumor and bureaucratic failure. Plenty of people hated her for exactly that.

"And you want me to do what?" Rae asked.

"Look into the matter before the story settles into something none of us can undo."

LaN's display brightened on her shoulder. "Detecting omission patterns," it chirped.

Blade ignored the remark, though not the implication. "You will have access to A.R.I. directly."

Rae studied him. "You say that as if it's a concession."

"It is."

Somewhere under the city, a machine designed to care for everyone had become the perfect container for blame. Rae did not trust A.R.I. She trusted the government even less. What she trusted was the look in Blade's face: not innocence, not honesty, but pressure.

He rose, crossed to the steel security door built into the side wall, and keyed it open.

It was not a storage room.

It was an elevator.

"Come along, then," Blade said.

Rae rose and followed him into the elevator.

Whatever had happened at Clona, she already disliked how neatly everyone seemed to be arranging it.
