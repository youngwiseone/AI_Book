# Chapter 3: Carter Is Recruited

**POV:** Carter  
**Story beat:** Carter is dragged back toward AI work through grief, memory, and obligation

He saw Chel in the moonlight again.

Her dark hair whipped around her face in the wind, and the white of her lab coat shone silver against the black sea behind her. She turned toward him smiling, one hand raised as if she were calling him closer, but the wind stole whatever words she meant to say. Then another voice somewhere beyond her caught her attention. Chel glanced toward the cliffs, took a step, lost her footing, and vanished.

Carter woke with a violent start and cracked his head on the underside of the transport vehicle he had been sleeping under.

"Aarrrgh, damn it!"

He dropped back onto the concrete, one hand over the lump already rising on his scalp. Beneath him, the workshop floor smelled the way it always had: oil, steel, solvent, dust, and old effort. The place was packed wall to wall with engines, tools, broken housings, spare parts, and half-finished repairs. A blind man could have stepped into it and named the profession in one breath.

He had opened the shop twenty years ago, back when his knees behaved and his beard had not yet started going gray out of spite. The building was older than it looked, all patched iron supports and concrete poured in different decades, but it held together the way good machinery did: not elegantly, just stubbornly. That suited him.

The dream was not new. It never was. Only the details changed.

He rubbed at his beard, found himself lying in a newly formed puddle of oil, and looked up in annoyance at the pipe above him. One quick twist with the wrench at his side settled it back into place.

With that done, the engine was finished too.

Time for coffee.

He dragged himself out from under the vehicle and levered his stiff body upright with much protest from his knees and back. On his way through the garage he hooked his wrench and tool belt back onto their places, paused in the washroom to inspect the red bump on his head, then wandered toward the kitchen in the small house attached to the shop.

The washroom had once been entirely ordinary. Carter had ruined that years ago by improving it in small, practical ways no sane person but him would appreciate. A squat mechanical scrubber sat beside the basin, built from a retired service motor, two brush heads, and a pedal switch welded out of scrap. He braced one boot on the pedal and let the brushes whir to life, rattling grease from his hands and forearms far faster than soap alone ever could. The thing made a terrible grinding sound, but it worked, which was the only praise Carter generally trusted.

Above the basin, the mirror had a crack in one corner and a thin strip of diagnostic lights he had installed to check skin tone for chemical exposure. Tonight they glowed a reassuring green. He leaned in closer, assessing the knot on his head and the oil streaked through his beard.

"Still hurts though," he muttered to his reflection. Now clean, Carter headed further into the house, reaching the kitchen.

The kitchen was hardly bigger than a decent storage cage, but it did the job. One old table. Two unmatched chairs. A bench stained by coffee rings and repair notes. A fruit bowl currently losing the argument against time. Across from him sat the empty chair Chel had once claimed as hers so completely that even now he never used it for anything except setting down parts he meant to sort later.

Above the bench, a row of salvaged magnetic strips held cutlery, spare screws, and one small socket wrench nobody had bothered to move back to the garage. The kitchen window looked onto the yard, where half-repaired chassis sat beneath patched tarps and rainwater gathered in old tyre rims. A service drone missing one arm slumped beside the fence waiting for parts, looking so dejected in the darkness that Carter had once threatened to paint a smile on it just to stop the thing lowering the mood.

While the kettle heated, he peered out the window to judge how long he had been asleep. It had been daylight when he started the repair. Now the yard outside was black.

Then a face appeared against the glass, grinning far too widely.

Carter jumped. "Dammit, Rupert, just knock, would you?"

The grin only widened. A moment later came the familiar pattern at the front door.

Tap. Ta-tap tap.

"Just let yourself in," Carter called.

Rupert entered with his usual unnecessary flourish, ducking under the slightly too-short doorframe in a gray suit that looked allergic to physical labor. He closed the door carefully behind him and brushed imaginary dust from one sleeve.

Rupert had grown into elegance the way some people grew into debt: gradually, then all at once. Carter still remembered him as a lanky kid in rolled sleeves, standing on a milk crate to reach the bench and complaining bitterly every time grease found its way under his nails.

He had hated the shop then too, or claimed to. Hated the smell, hated the grime, hated that Carter kept old bolts sorted in coffee tins with handwritten labels instead of something sleek and indexed and civilized. Yet he had kept turning up after school, then after work, then whenever life elsewhere had become too loud. Chel used to say Rupert complained the way other people prayed: often, dramatically, and in the hope that somebody competent would intervene.

Carter dropped into his chair with his mug and set another in front of Rupert.

"As graceful as ever, I see," Rupert said, eyeing Carter's oil-stained shirt and the swelling knot on his head.

"Been a while since you've visited the shop," Carter said. "Thought maybe you were ready to get those hands dirty again."

Rupert looked faintly offended by the very idea. "I am pleased to say that is not the matter I have come to discuss."

He took a cautious sip of the coffee, made a face as though Carter had handed him solvent by mistake, and pushed the mug several inches away from himself.

"What brings you here then?" Carter asked, retrieving Rupert's abandoned cup and pouring it into his own.

Rupert reached into his satchel and placed a sealed file on the table.

Clona.

The name alone was enough to sour the room.

"I was told to ask for your help regarding A.R.I.," Rupert said.

Carter nearly choked on the coffee. He coughed, splattered some across the table, and swore as Rupert recoiled in horror for the sake of his suit.

"What? You want me to look at an AI?" Carter barked. "You know how much I dislike that artificial nonsense."

"I know it comes as a surprise," Rupert said, dabbing uselessly at his sleeve, "but I was told that if anything happened with A.R.I. that was out of the ordinary, I was to come to you."

"Which idiot told you that?"

Rupert hesitated only long enough to make Carter's stomach tighten.

"Chel."

The room went still.

Carter turned away first, pretending to check the biscuit tin so Rupert would not have to watch his face. Of course it would be Chel. Of course the machine at the center of this mess would be the one she had poured years of herself into. He set the tin on the table and sat down more slowly than before.

"Why?" he asked.

Rupert opened the file, then added one more thing to the table beside it: Chel's old diagnostic key, scratched along one edge, unmistakable.

"Because she said you wouldn't need to understand the coding," Rupert said quietly. "You understand how she thought. If something in A.R.I. has been built wrong, hidden wrong, or forced to act wrong, you'd feel where the shape is off."

He slid the file closer. Inside were printouts, stills from the blast, casualty estimates, and two pages of sanitized government language trying very hard not to sound frightened. One photograph showed the broken shell of the Clona factory under floodlights, emergency crews moving through the wreckage in reflective coats while drones hovered above with sterile blue optics. Another showed a line of citizens behind temporary barriers, faces pale in the artificial glare.

Carter stared at the key.

Clona. Hundreds dead. A.R.I. taking the blame. Chel's voice reaching out through someone else's mouth years too late.

He hated A.I. for wearing human manners. Hated the government for treating intelligence like property. Hated that one mention of Chel was still enough to move him.

"I don't know anything about software ghosts," he muttered.

"You know broken things," Rupert said. "And this looks broken on purpose."

Carter took the key in his grease-blackened hand and let the familiar weight settle there.

"This is a bad idea," he said.

"Almost certainly."

Carter sighed, reached for the biscuit tin, and flipped the lid open. Rupert immediately leaned in and selected an afghan with the self-possession of a man who had rejected the coffee but intended to survive the visit somehow.

Carter chose a snickerdoodle large enough to qualify as a meal, bit into it, and pointed the rest vaguely at Rupert. "Fine. I'll look. But if this turns into political theatre, I'm out."

Relief crossed Rupert's face so quickly he almost hid it.

"I will take that as a yes, then," Rupert said, sounding relieved enough to be irritating.

"Not for free, but yesh, I will dough what I can," Carter muttered through a mouthful of biscuit.

Rupert rolled his eyes. "Of course, Carter. I am sure the government can survive compensating you for your charming professionalism."

"It'll be a first."

"We can meet tomorrow," Rupert said. "Nine o'clock. The new Bio-Cafe around the corner."

"I'll be late."

"I already accounted for that."

Neither of them moved to end the visit straight away. With the worst of the conversation out of the way, they slipped into older habits as easily as if no years had passed. Rupert talked about the new place he was renting and how the kitchen there now spoke directly to his personal assistant system, planning meals before he'd even decided whether he was hungry. Carter called that lazy, unnatural nonsense, then spent ten full minutes describing a giant slug he had found under a busted transport rig the week before, broad as a dinner plate and unreasonably confident for something without bones.

Rupert argued that automated meal planning was a triumph of civilization. Carter argued that if a kitchen needed to talk to a bot before a man could fry an egg, civilization had taken a wrong turn somewhere. Rupert retaliated by observing that Carter lived alone with an automatic grease scrubber, a service drone with no arm, and enough spare engine parts to build a small religion.

"A useful religion," Carter said.

"A noisy one."

Outside, a freight tram rumbled past at the end of the lane, shaking the window just enough to stir the spoons hanging on the magnetic strip. Somewhere farther off, one of the night-market vendors was still calling the last of their stock through a cheap speaker. The city never really slept. It only changed shifts.

Carter listened to Rupert talk and let the familiar rhythm of it settle around the room. For a little while it almost felt like older evenings, the kind where Chel would come in late with too many tabs open in her head, steal half of Carter's biscuit without asking, and tell Rupert his latest life plan was ridiculous in a tone so affectionate it made the insult useless. Memory sat in the kitchen with them now as tangibly as the mugs on the table.

Rupert laughed despite himself, one hand pressed to his chest as if the sound had surprised him on the way out. For a little while the room felt less empty.

When Rupert finally stood to leave, he shrugged back into his jacket and glanced once, briefly, toward Chel's chair before looking away.

"Thank you for having me," he said. "And for agreeing to help."

Carter walked him to the door. "Yeah, yeah. Get out before I make you take some more of the coffee with you."

Rupert ducked under the frame, smiling now in a quieter way. "See you tomorrow."

For the first time that evening, Carter smiled properly.
