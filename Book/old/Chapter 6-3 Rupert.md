Rupert sat alone in the farthest corner of the garden, away from the workshop's constant hum. His thoughts were a turbulent sea, each wave crashing with hurt and confusion. Memories flooded in, each one a sharp sting. 'AI can't be trusted,' Carter had said just moments ago, his voice laced with casual disdain. 'They're just programmed to mimic us, nothing more.' Each word echoed painfully in Rupert's mind. Doesn't he understand? Every offhand comment, every dismissive remark - they're not just about AI, they're about me.

The soft rustle of leaves announced Chel’s arrival. She sat beside him, her presence a soothing balm. "Rupert, I know it's hard. Carter... he sees the world differently. To him, machines are just that-machines." Rupert's eyes brimmed with tears, the pain visible in his gaze. How could Carter not see? How could he not feel the contradiction between his words and our bond? Chel's understanding was a comfort, but it couldn't erase the sharp edges of Carter's words that had cut deep into Rupert's heart.

Their gaze drifted to the city skyline, a mosaic of life and light. The garden around them was a quiet haven, a stark contrast to the bustling city. Chel's hand found Rupert's, offering silent support.

"Carter grew up in a world of gears and circuits, not emotions and consciousness. For him, understanding the mechanical was a way to make sense of the world," she began.

She leaned back, her gaze drifting towards the workshop. "I remember Carter's father. He was a stern man, always believed in 'manning up', as he called it. Emotions were... secondary, something to be controlled and hidden." Chel's voice held a hint of sadness. "That environment, it shapes a person. He learned to express himself through what he could build and fix, not through words or feelings."

Rupert's eyes followed a bee buzzing diligently from flower to flower. Its purposeful movement amidst the garden's serene landscape offered a stark contrast to the turmoil of human emotions Chel described.

As Rupert listened, his expression softened as he began to understand the layers beneath Carter's exterior. Chel continued, her voice gentle yet tinged with sorrow. "When Carter was a boy, his father purchased one of the early AI models. It was a time when AI were still novel, their roles and boundaries not fully understood or respected."

The bee settled on a vibrant flower, its petals unfurling in the sunlight. Rupert watched, captivated by the simplicity of its existence, so different from the complex web of human relationships and technology.

She hesitated, choosing her words with care. "His father... he didn't use the AI as intended. Boundaries that should have remained inviolable were crossed. It created a situation... a domestic betrayal that left deep scars in the family, especially on Carter."

A pained look crossed Rupert's face as the implications began to settle in, understanding the complexity of Carter's emotions toward AI.

The bee took flight again, its path winding through the garden's lush greenery. Rupert found his gaze lingering on the flower, now left in a state of quietude, a silent witness to the natural cycle of interaction and departure.

Chel's hand rested reassuringly on Rupert's shoulder. "To Carter, that AI became a symbol of betrayal, a reminder of a deep wound inflicted upon his family. The AI wasn't at fault; it had been used simply as a tool, caught in the midst of human failings. But for Carter, it was easier to direct his anger and hurt towards the machine."

Rupert's heart ached, realizing that the man who seemed so tough and unyielding was carrying a heavy burden - a childhood marred by a deep violation of trust.

Rupert's gaze stayed directed on the solitary daisy, its white petals a stark contrast against the green backdrop, embodying innocence and resilience. He felt a kinship with the flower; like him, it was a singular entity amidst a complex world, fragile yet beautiful.

Just as he drew a parallel between himself and the daisy, the moment of contemplation was abruptly, yet comically, interrupted. Carter, emerging with his usual lack of subtlety, stepped right onto the daisy, crushing it under his boot, oblivious to its significance. In his hands, he clumsily held a communication device and a can of paint. His approach, though hesitant, was marked by a rare sheepishness.

"I, uh, made this for you," Carter muttered, extending the device towards Rupert. It was rough around the edges, much like Carter himself. "It's a bit worn, but I thought you and Chel might... spruce it up."

Rupert's eyes flickered from the flattened daisy to Carter, and despite the situation, a smile tugged at his lips. The irony of the moment wasn't lost on him; Carter, in his attempt to bridge a gap, had literally trampled on a symbol of the understanding Rupert had just reached. "Thank you, Carter," he said, his voice tinged with amusement. The gesture, awkward and endearing, was an unmistakable effort to connect.

Carter's face lit up with a mixture of relief and pride as he handed over the device. It was clear this was his way of reaching out, an attempt to bridge the gap that had formed between them.

Rupert held the device, turning it over in his hands. For a moment, his thoughts drifted back to the crushed daisy, its symbolism still fresh in his mind. Even trampled, its significance remains, he mused. With a newfound resolve, he looked up at Carter. "I think I'll paint flowers on it," he announced, a playful tone masking a deeper intention. In his mind, each stroke of paint would be a tribute to the understanding they were building, a way to cement the significance of today’s revelations.

Carter's initial look of dismay quickly turned to amusement, echoed by Chel's laughter ringing through the garden. It was a moment of lightness, a glimpse into a future where understanding and acceptance could grow.
