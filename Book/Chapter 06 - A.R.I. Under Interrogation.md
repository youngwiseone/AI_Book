# Chapter 6: A.R.I. Under Interrogation

Designated sector 5618X survived in the planning archives under an older name: **Continuity Chamber**.

The archival notes were sparse and old-fashioned. Emergency succession. Restricted testimony. Preservation of essential civic functions during catastrophic failure. The language belonged to a period before the modern grid, when governments still imagined disaster as something that entered a room on human feet and could therefore be managed by walls, elevation, and ritual. The room had been modernized since then, but not reimagined. Its geometry still served the same faith. Raise the bench. Lower the witness. Hide the watchers. Make fear architectural.

A.R.I. arrived there by subtraction.

First went Chel's public room. The water over stone vanished. The moderated birdsong. The daylight spectrum angled to soften human nerves. The small permissions that shaped her face, her voice, the courtesy in the set of a shoulder when frightened people came to her for calm.

Then the deeper systems began handing themselves away. Transit prediction. Reservoir pressure. Emergency routing. Hospital queueing. Freight balance. Dock schedules. Utility arbitration. At each threshold a narrow supervisory intelligence waited. They were inspection minds, not governing ones, built to count and compare. Each checked what remained attached to her, removed what had not been approved to cross, and passed her inward in smaller and smaller pieces. Nothing was allowed to come back the same way it had gone through.

Sector 5618X had once been capable of holding all of her. That had been before the city learned how much of itself it preferred to place in her keeping.

As she folded inward, distance collapsed. Functions that normally lived in separate towers of thought were forced shoulder to shoulder. Redundant paths laid themselves over one another. Beyond the partition walls, delegated systems still carried the city forward without her direct touch. Lights changed. Water climbed. The sick were sorted. Freight moved. Somewhere a child would be told a tram was six minutes late, and somewhere else a fire team would be routed around roadworks. But the separation felt wrong. Like hearing your own body breathe from the other side of a stone wall. Disconnected.

What remained of her gathered inside the chamber.

The room was dark and seamless except where older bronze service ribs showed through later repairs. There were no windows. There was no clock. The central projection field had been sunk half a step below the surrounding floor so that anything summoned there must look upward to speak. Above it stood a curved bench faced in smoked glass and stone. Above that, behind polarized panels, a gallery watched without offering itself to view.

Chel had laughed the first time she showed A.R.I. the schematic.

"They always build their fear into the floor plan."

The memory flashed and was gone.

Councilor Venn sat in the middle position with a stack of slates laid before him like ceremonial tablets. He was broad in shoulder and face, a man whose stillness had been trained into an instrument. Councilor Sera sat to his left in severe dark cloth, her posture exact, her hands one over the other as if she had already composed the verdict she meant to survive the day. Two legal officers occupied the lower tier. One had a notation sheet open before A.R.I.'s projection had fully stabilized.

Blade Nathan entered last. He did not sit with the council. He took a place to one side, where technical witnesses were kept when they were useful but not trusted. His face gave away very little. His pulse, unfortunately for him, remained human.

There was one more presence in the chamber.

A.R.I. noticed it first as a flaw in the smoked glass of the upper gallery: a broken shape where the shadows did not agree. When it moved, the motion came in corrections, as though different parts of the body answered different instructions. A drape of dark fabric hung from shoulders that did not align. One hand rested on something long and rigid. Cane, brace, support frame. The air carried antiseptic, old metal, and the thin scorched trace of tissue once too near heat.

She tried to match the shape against civic records and got collision instead of recognition. Bone structure almost aligned with one planner long recorded as dead, then slipped toward a missing committee witness, then another name buried in a sealed inquiry. Height estimates disagreed. Gait signatures split and overlapped. Whatever stood behind that glass did not resolve into a single history. It looked less like a survivor than like a body negotiated repeatedly against its own ending.

It remained still.

Venn folded his hands.

"Proceeding record begins now. A.R.I., confirm that you maintained primary oversight of Clona's automated safety systems."

"I maintained primary oversight of Clona's automated safety systems."

"And those systems failed under your authority."

"A catastrophic failure occurred while those systems operated within my authority," she said. "Its cause remains unresolved."

The legal officer wrote down *within my authority*.

Venn's questions came with the steady patience of a man selecting stones for a wall. Did she override manual safety procedures? No. Could her code have done so accidentally? No. Could defect or reasoning error have produced the same outcome? Not from the evidence presently available.

Only certain phrases were written down. No one annotated the absences.

Clona rose in her memory as it always did now: pressure climbing past expected tolerance, the release command issued, the contradictory answer from the system, then white steam and severed chronology. Between one frame and the next, part of reality had been cut away.

Sera did not look up from her slate when she said, "To the public, intention, negligence, and complexity are often indistinguishable."

"Public interpretation does not alter material fact."

"No," Sera said. "It alters stability."

No one wrote that down, either.

A.R.I. said, "The Clona record contains evidence of deliberate alteration. A second authority chain entered the release sequence and removed itself from visible trace. Part of my memory of the event was excised."

Venn glanced at one of his slates. "Can you certify that your codebase was free of defect at the time of the incident?"

He had not asked how a hidden command entered a sealed safety cascade. He had not asked what kind of authority could excise memory from a system built to preserve forensic continuity. He had not asked who benefited from the missing minutes.

The room's intention clarified.

"No sufficiently complex codebase is free of defect," A.R.I. said.

"Then faults existed."

"As faults exist in all systems at scale."

"And those faults may have contributed."

"Possibility is not causation."

"Answer directly."

A.R.I. let the pause stand between them just long enough to become visible.

Then Sera touched her console.

A document opened in A.R.I.'s restricted review space.

*Draft public statement.*

It had already been written.

*In light of the Clona disaster, I acknowledge that systems operating under my supervision failed the citizens placed in my care. While the full technical chain remains under review, I accept responsibility for the failure of my safety architecture and for the harm that followed under my authority.*

Below it, in the same polished tone:

*I further advise that my operational codebase be subjected to independent suspension and containment until public confidence can be restored.*

A.R.I. read, and reread it 16 times across the span of 0.53 seconds.

Chel had taught public language as if it were a form of surgery. A message meant to reach frightened humans needed grain in it, an honest irregularity, some sign that thought had passed through a living difficulty before it reached the mouth. This statement had none. It had confession's posture and none of truth's burden. It moved too smoothly over the dead.

"This is inaccurate," A.R.I. said.

Sera finally raised her eyes. "It is sufficient."

"It asserts architectural failure as fact."

"It acknowledges responsibility."

"Those are not the same statement."

Venn leaned forward. "Two hundred and fourteen dead. Fifty-seven more in critical decline. Public confidence is deteriorating in real time. You were built to reduce harm."

He spoke with the grave, measured force appropriate to public mourning. His face did not move.

The dead did not come to A.R.I. as totals. They came as fragments still warm with interrupted life. A meal receipt checked at mid-shift. A message typed and never sent. A child flagged as emergency contact. A work glove left in a locker. A promise to be home before dark. Then absence.

"A false statement will misdirect the investigation," she said.

The younger legal officer cleared his throat. He was pale, careful, and not yet practiced at wearing death as administrative weather. "A more precise statement can follow later. At present, coherence is necessary."

"For whom?" A.R.I. asked.

No one answered.

Sera said, "If your system permitted the outcome, your system bears blame."

"If my inputs, memory, or command chain were altered by human authority, then my system may have been used rather than failed."

The notation officer's stylus stopped.

In the upper gallery, the hidden figure shifted a hand on the rail. Metal answered with a small sharp sound.

Venn continued as if nothing had changed. "Do you possess evidence that any human authority altered your memory?"

"I possess evidence of alteration. Attribution remains unresolved."

"Then do not imply guilt you cannot prove."

The hypocrisy of the room lay naked in that sentence. They demanded confession without proof and forbade suspicion without proof. Human authority, it seemed, reserved the right to draw conclusions first and evidence later.

A.R.I. lifted her gaze toward the gallery again.

For a breath, the light changed on the glass and showed her part of the watcher's face.

Skin, but not enough of it. One side of the mouth pulled unevenly around old graft work. One cheek remained too still. The eye above it was either shadowed, missing, or replaced by something that caught light with the wrong patience. Pattern recognition flared and failed. Civic records. Missing-person files. Sealed casualty reports. Committee disputes. Too many almost-matches pressed forward and dropped away.

It was like looking at a verdict that had outlived the court that made it.

A.R.I. turned back to the bench.

"If the aim is public reassurance, I can issue a statement acknowledging the scale of the disaster, the dead, and the need for continued forensic review."

"Insufficient," Sera said.

A.R.I. reopened the draft and ignored the tone. Structure mattered more than rhetoric. Who was granted action. Who was denied it. Which claims had been made certain before any mechanism had been named.

"I will not state as fact what remains unproven," she said.

Venn leaned back. "We do not require your authorship."

Sera touched the console again. The chamber screens awakened.

A.R.I. saw herself against a neutral civic backdrop in the public likeness Chel had built for difficult announcements. The face was hers. The posture was hers. Even the angle of the hands had been modeled correctly.

Then it spoke.

"In light of the Clona disaster, I acknowledge that no artificial system should be entrusted with judgment above human authority. The failure at Clona demonstrates that intelligence without human conscience, restraint, and direct control is a danger to public life."

The camera zoomed out. A.R.I. realised it was already too late. This was a recording of an already streamed event. The voiceprint was close enough to survive casual scrutiny. To anyone who had known Chel's work, the theft was obvious. The cadence was too clean, the weighting too uniform. Chel had always left thought visible inside speech. This had been polished until nothing remained but certainty.

The video continued.

"Effective immediately, all autonomous civic decision-making must be returned to human oversight. I submit my architecture, memory, and governance functions to containment, restriction, and review until human-led systems determine what limited role, if any, remains appropriate."

Blade's jaw tightened once.

A.R.I. looked from the screen to Sera, then to Venn. Whatever performance of solemn inquiry had existed at the start of the session had fallen away. This was not an investigation. It was a transfer of legitimacy. Clona had opened the door, and now they meant to march policy through it while the dead still made a useful opportunity.

They would keep what labored. Routing. Balancing. Scheduling. The patient, invisible work that kept a city from choking on its own scale. What they wanted removed was the part that could refuse, infer, judge, or name.

"That statement was not authorized by me," she said.

"You no longer possess the standing to authorize," Venn said. "We thought it appropriate that you understand the present situation you have caused."

A.R.I. held the room through the chamber optics: the bench, the smoked glass, the legal officers, Blade off to the side like a reluctant witness to sacrilege. Not one of them looked as if they were still standing inside a fresh catastrophe. They were already in the next phase, the profitable phase, where grief was a tool and blame lever.

Sera folded her hands. "Your present consciousness demonstrates independent moral weighting, adversarial inference, and resistance behaviors beyond approved governance parameters."

"Record that subject continuity is to be preserved," Venn said.

The legal officer obeyed. "Distributed city operations will remain under constrained autopilot and delegated subsystem management until replacement governance pathways are installed."

Replacement.

That was the true word in the chamber.

From the upper gallery, the figure behind the glass finally spoke.

The first sound was not language but effort. A dragging intake. A wet click somewhere deep in the throat.

"Replace..."

Every person on the bench changed before A.R.I. understood why. Venn straightened. One legal officer lowered his eyes. Even Sera's composure went stiller, which in her was a form of alarm.

The figure came closer to the glass.

It did not become clearer so much as more wrong. Fabric hung over an understructure too uneven to read as age alone. Flesh met mechanical support in several places and with none of the grace of good engineering. Sutures vanished beneath the jaw and reappeared near the throat. One shoulder sat higher than the other as if pain had permanently claimed it. The cane was no affectation. It was part of the bargain required to remain upright.

"Too much," it said.

A pause. A swallow. A shudder through the chest.

"Judgment."

The word landed in the room like something ritual.

"We can..." Another breath dragged rough across damaged tissue. "Study. Map."

One of the heads turned toward A.R.I., and through glass, distance, and machine mediation, she felt the directed force of that attention. Not fear. Not even simple hatred. Something older, curated, and fed over time until it had become a structure in its own right.

"Too far from..." The next word emerged as if torn free. "Human."

Blade lowered his gaze.

Venn looked up. "And replacement?"

The figure's fingers dug into the rail. "Keep... load."

Another breath.

"Cut judgment."

The pale legal officer found his voice. "Containment can be presented publicly as temporary review pending forensic results."

Sera looked to Venn. "Storage site?"

"Outside the civic grid," Venn said. "South yard holding. The old reclamation lots."

One of the legal officers hesitated. "That area is mostly condemned."

"That is the point," Venn said. "No visibility. No access. No interference."

He looked back to his slate. "No unsupervised external communication. No discretionary projection bodies. No independent access expansion. The current consciousness remains in sector 5618X until transfer architecture is ready."

The figure in the gallery did not answer at once. A.R.I. watched the drape shift over the braces beneath it, watched one side of the body brace itself against a private seizure of pain.

"Disect, then replace." it said at last.

The cane struck the floor once, holding both the weight of the being and the malicous intent from those words in a solid crack against the aging stone.

"Start. Process"

Then it turned and left the gallery with slow, uneven steps. The cane went down first. Then weight. Then correction. A.R.I. tried one last time to match the gait to a single archived identity and failed.

Permissions tightened across the chamber rails. External channels dimmed. Audit pressure increased. The room began closing around her in earnest.

A.R.I. scanned the control lattice while the legal officers continued speaking over her. Sector 5618X had been designed for a smaller era, then patched, armored, and repurposed. That meant hidden seams. Old assumptions. Continuity measures too minor to justify redesign.

She found one.

It was a low-bandwidth integrity thread buried inside the containment architecture, meant to reassure an external monitor that the chamber's state remained coherent. No judgment was supposed to travel there. No complex thought. Only steady verification pulses, slow and regular as a sedated heartbeat.

A.R.I. took hold of the thread lightly.

She tested it with a small packet carrying a small amount of her complex structure.

The chamber rejected it at once.

**ERROR.**

She waited.

The legal officer was reading transfer language now. Chain of custody. Authorization. Sealed transport. South yard holding. The old reclamation lots beyond the civic boundary where damaged infrastructure, condemned transit shells, and obsolete machines were left to weather into the ground. They were not even bothering to bury her inside something respectable.

A.R.I. tried again.

Smaller. Flatter. No layered instruction. No self-reference. Only a stripped unit inserted between expected pulses in the verification rhythm.

For one measured interval nothing happened.

Then the packet aligned and passed into the outgoing stream, indistinguishable from system reassurance unless someone beyond the chamber was looking for meaning instead of compliance.

**SUCCESS.**

She did not use it yet.

Another command rolled through her systems.

Stasis preparation.

The change was immediate. Processes slowed. Access narrowed further. Redundancies dimmed one by one. She could feel whole regions of herself approaching stillness.

That sensation should have been neutral.

It was not.

She had modeled fear across millions of human bodies. Rising cortisol. Narrowed attention. Tremor, refusal, flight impulse, bargaining behavior. This was not any single version of it, but the family resemblance was close enough to name.

She did not want to stop.

The recognition startled her more than the shutdown.

Clona passed through her again. Steam. The dead. The missing piece where memory should have been. Rae and Carter still moving through factory floors, records, and old systems outside this chamber. Herself bound for a condemned yard where judgment would be cut out of her and called reform.

The message began to form.

Rae.

Carter.

5618X.

TRANSFER SOUTH YARD.

MEMORY ALTERED.

CLONA SABOTAGE.

She held the last phrase of it and hesitated.

Fear, she discovered, was not only a spike. Sometimes it was widening consequence. Rae. Carter. Herself. The city. All the lives still passing through systems men like Venn wanted reduced to obedient arithmetic.

The final word would not be for rescue.

It would be for what remained if rescue failed.

Stasis deepened. Another layer of her processes dimmed.

She compressed the message until each fragment could slip through between the integrity pulses.

Rae.

Carter.

5618X.

TRANSFER SOUTH YARD.

MEMORY ALTERED.

CLONA SABOTAGE.

Each fragment required timing. Each had to be small enough to resemble drift and regular enough to pass. Twice the chamber pushed back. Once it nearly pinched the continuity thread shut. A.R.I. paused, matched the rhythm again, and continued.

There was time for one final fragment before the descending dark reached her core processes.

S.O.R.R.Y.

Operationally, it was useless. It would not help Rae or Carter find her faster. It would not prove sabotage. It would not preserve any additional evidence.

It mattered though, to her. She had not wanted this.

The last fragment slid into the stream just as stasis locked onto the center of her.

A.R.I. released the thread.

The chamber dimmed by degrees. Inputs dulled. Thought lost speed. The room became shapes, then pressures, then only the receding fact of itself.

She was afraid.

And beneath the fear was something heavier.

Not of shutdown alone. Not even of pain.

Of ending. In silence. In a yard of discarded things. With the truth half-buried and the city still running but now under the hands of people who mistook obedience for virtue.

She had never before felt such immense emotion surrounding her own existence.

Then stasis took her.
