# Chapter 7: The City Turns

**POV:** Rae  
**Story beat:** The investigation spills into public unrest as Rae and Carter realize Clona is being used to reshape who holds power in the city

By the time Rae reached the civic square, evening had turned it into a wound lit for display.

The daylight had drained out of the high glass towers, leaving them silver at the edges and black at the core. Memorial cloth hung between polished facades. Projected condolence ribbons drifted over stone colonnades. The long mourning wall raised against the east side of the square was already crowded with flowers, candles, paper names, and low-resolution family photos lifted from old social feeds. Half of it had been covered by placards.

Placards rose and dipped over the crowd in uneven currents. `A.R.I. Saved Us.` `Clona Deserved Better.` `Shut Her Down.` `Human Hands Back on the Grid.` `No More Bio Lies.` `No Machine Gods.` The hand-painted ones shook in tired fingers. The printed ones were straighter, cleaner, better funded. Rae distrusted those immediately.

The square itself had become several cities at once.

Families from the factory districts stood close to the mourning wall with black ribbons tied around their sleeves and grief sharpened into accusation. Young systems workers and med-techs clustered near the transit steps arguing that A.R.I. had kept half the city alive for years and was being framed now. Vendors who should have been packing up for the night still worked their carts in place, unwilling to lose a shift simply because history had chosen their square as a stage. Behind them all, smaller knots of men and women in matching gray jackets moved through the crowd passing out professionally printed signs and outrage with equal efficiency.

LaN tightened his grip on Rae's shoulder. His display flickered with motion flags only she could see.

"Current estimate: one thousand three hundred and twelve people," he said. "Twenty-three probable agitators. Sixteen armed security. One pastry cart maintaining admirable discipline."

"I respect the professionalism under pressure." Rae noted, looking across at the pastry cart.

The pastry cart stood at the edge of the square under a strip light, the vendor still selling fried dough twists to whoever could remain ideological while eating sugar. Capitalism it seems was able to pull realism into an otherwise unwordly scene. The banners or the shouting, made the whole area feel abstract, like in a seperate space. But, cities did not stop for crisis. And pastry it seems did not stop for anyone. She laughed at the absurdity of anyone buying food in a time like this.

Above the square, the civic holo-murals had given up pretending this was an ordinary evening. What should have been artful cityscapes and public health advisories now cycled between council seals, casualty tallies, and formal lines of condolence in expensive type. Every image lingered a fraction longer on the council crest than on the dead.

A voice rolled out over the public speakers.

"Pending forensic review of the Clona event, higher-order autonomous civic governance functions are being placed under interim human oversight. Residents are advised to remain calm, comply with security directives, and report anomalous machine behavior to authorized personnel."

The phrasing was polished enough to sound inevitable. That made Rae dislike it more.

LaN said quietly, "Transit synchronization across the central district has degraded by eleven percent in the last twenty minutes."

Rae looked up. "That fast?"

"Yes. Routing arbitration is being pulled back from the automated layer. Several intersections are now awaiting human approval stacks."

As if the city wanted to prove the point, a horn began blaring somewhere beyond the square. Then another. The two notes tangled into one long animal complaint while traffic stalled at an upper crossing.

Carter stopped beside her, munching on a warm pastry bearing the logo of the stand she had spotted earlier, expression hard enough to chip stone.

"That wasn't her," he said between mouthfuls, staring out ahead toward the crowd gathered.

Rae glanced at him. "You mean the statement."

"I mean the whole performance." He kept his eyes on the nearest screen, where A.R.I.'s public likeness had been frozen into a solemn profile beside the casualty count. "Too clean. Too complete. Too quickly useful."

"You've spoken to her once."

"I've spoken to Chel's work my whole life." His jaw tightened. "Chel built space into hard messages. Room for thought. Room for doubt. Whoever wrote that statement didn't want either. They wanted control."

Rae looked back at the screen.

Control was the right word.

What had been released into the square was not just blame. It was doctrine. The council had not merely told the city that A.R.I. might have failed. It had blamed and told the civillians just what kind of future would follow. Human first. Human oversight. Human conscience restored by committee, "Bah!" as if committees had a conscience when they only needed jurisdiction.

A chant rose from the western side of the square and hit the stone hard enough to come back uglier.

"AI must die! AI must die! AI must die!"

The matching gray-jacketed group had reached the base of the fountain. Their signs were not handmade, not hurriedly printed in grief or panic. They were uniform in dimension, color, and slogan. Someone had paid for them before sunset. Someone had expected the city to be ready for a simpler argument than truth. She told LaN to make a note of this, then tucked him beneath her coat. Whatever was going on here was not rational, he needed to stay out of site.

A younger woman near the mourning wall turned on the gray mass with such force Rae thought she might strike somebody.

She wore a Clona worker badge still clipped to her coat. Her eyes were red-rimmed and bloodless with fatigue.

"My brother was on second shift," she shouted. "You don't get to turn him into your campaign!"

One of the gray jackets answered without missing a beat. "And who ran the system that killed him?"

"I trust A.R.I. she has helped so many I know. She has been running the city 7 years now. Even if this was a mistake.." She trailed off, unsure she wanted to finish the sentence. Tiredness and immense sorrow clung it's weight to her tiny frame.

"Conspiracy! Hearsay! Don't let machines fool you into obendiance." They pushed past her and onwards with their chant. "AI must die!"

The words were neat. Prewritten. They passed through the square with the dead, synthetic ease of something rehearsed in a room far quieter than this one.

On the opposite side of the fountain, a tram supervisor in reflective orange argued with an older man holding a `Shut Her Down` placard.

"You pull A.R.I. off the grid in one afternoon and see what happens," the supervisor snapped. "My whole district is already behind. Dispatch is blind."

The older man jabbed his sign toward the casualty numbers overhead. "Blind? Two hundred and fourteen dead sounds blind to me."

"It sounds like sabotage."

"It sounds like you trusted a machine, robot sympathisor." He spat the words out.

No one in the square was outright lying, Rae thought. That was what made it dangerous. The grief was real. The fear was real. The dependence was real. People whose families had been protected by A.R.I.'s systems for years now had to stand beside people whose families had died under those same systems.

Then Rae saw one slogan again and felt the old cold line of it under her ribs.

`No More Bio Lies.`

The sign was being held by a clean-shaven man in a civic volunteer vest, the kind of face bureaucracies preferred because it looked harmless from ten meters away. Behind him stood two women with `Human Hands Back on the Grid` signs. The phrases had no necessary connection except fear itself, but power had always liked bundling its enemies. Machine judgment. Modified flesh. Systems too complex for committees to bully cleanly. Tell frightened people the borders are dissolving and they will beg you for someone to draw them again.

Blade's office came back to her for a moment. The syringe. The scales at his throat. The endless soft language that always surrounded engineered bodies until the bill came due in pain.

Rae touched the scar above her lip with the back of one finger and dropped her hand before Carter could notice.

"You know that slogan crowd?" he asked, reading her face anyway.

"I know the type. They always say `human` when they mean approved influence. No way the will allow just any person to be in charge."

LaN's optics narrowed. "Additional note: the gray-jacket group entered the square from three different access points at almost the same time."

"Organized," Rae said.

"Deliberately so."

The public screens changed again.

For a moment the council seal held. Then A.R.I.'s face appeared beside it, soft-lit and composed, the exact likeness Rae had seen down in the Hive and already mistrusted here.

"Citizens of Clona and the greater central districts," the broadcast said, "a period of controlled transition is now in effect. Essential machine functions will continue under revised human supervision until further notice."

The crowd reacted as if struck along different nerves at once. Cheers from the gray jackets. Boos from the systems workers. Sobs from somewhere near the wall. A bottle sailed out of the crowd and shattered against the edge of the fountain.

That was enough.

Security moved immediately, too quickly to have been merely alert. Black-armored officers advanced from both sides of the square with crowd shields already extended, driving a wedge toward the broken glass. The movement compressed everybody else. Bodies surged. Placards snapped. Someone screamed that the machine was fighting back. Someone else screamed that the council had staged the whole thing. Someone nearby started shouting for a medic before anyone had even fallen.

LaN projected a spray of red markers across Rae's connected vision lens.

"Agitators repositioning. Probability of crush event rising."

"We need to move." She called out to Carter, but he had already dissapeared into the crowd. Then she heard the child crying.

The sound came thin and high from somewhere near the fountain's lower steps. A little girl in a yellow rain jacket had been spun away from her mother by the security push and now stood trapped between a dropped placard and a man's knees, crying hard enough she could not shout a name.

Rae cut toward her.

Someone slammed into Rae's shoulder. She drove back with her hip, caught the child under one arm, and pivoted before the next body could close the space. The mother was three meters away, white with panic, trying to force herself upstream through a wave of shouting strangers.

"Here!" Rae shouted.

The mother never heard her.

Carter did.

He came through the crowd like a piece of industrial equipment given motive force. He did not waste time on finesse. He put a hand on one protester's shoulder, another on a security barrier, and opened space by the simple expedient of refusing to move backward when the square wanted him to. Two people tried to shout at him. One look at his face made them choose easier enemies.

"This way!" he barked.

Rae shoved the child into the pocket of air he created. The mother lurched forward and caught the girl so hard they nearly went down together. Rae braced both of them until they found their feet.

"Take the south steps," she said. "Don't stop in the transit arch. Keep moving until you're out of the square."

The mother nodded without actually processing the words and stumbled away holding the child high against her chest.

Near the fountain rim, a young man in a systems-union patch had gone down under the rush. One of the gray jackets stepped back instead of helping him, preserving his clean sign above the crush. Carter swore, shoved the sign carrier sideways with enough force to send him spinning, and hauled the fallen man upright by the front of his coat.

"You all right?"

"No," the man gasped.

"Too Bad. Move." Carter pushed him onwards, toward a northern exit from the square.

Rae caught the brief expression on the gray jacket's face as security advanced past him. It was not fear. It was irritation at disrupted choreography.

The line broke, reformed, and broke again. A flood of people spilled toward the tram concourse. Others climbed onto the base of the mourning wall to shout over everyone else. The candles there tipped and went out one by one beneath trampling feet and dragging coats, sending up little curls of bitter smoke that vanished into the larger stink of sweat, synth-fabric, rain-damp stone, and adrenaline.

Rae found Carter again near the edge of the transit stairs. He was breathing harder now but looked only angrier for it.

"You see that?" he said.

"Which part?"

"The part where the security line waited until the bottle flew and then moved exactly where the camera drones already were."

Rae looked up.

Three news drones hovered over the square in perfect triangular spacing, their lenses turned toward the fountain, the flags, the pushing bodies, the kind of image that would let commentators say `public disorder`.

"Yes," she said. "I saw it."

Below them the public speakers repeated the same notice in a calmer tone, as if calm were a thing one could impose by volume and better diction.

Rae followed Carter down the transit steps and out of the densest part of the crowd. The air under the concourse roof felt no cleaner, but at least it moved. Commuters packed the platform in uneven clusters, all of them staring up at schedule boards that now flickered with delay notices and route substitutions every few seconds.

LaN said, "Manual review backlog has spread into metro dispatch, municipal freight timing, and emergency corridor assignments."

"Meaning?" Carter asked.

"Meaning the city is already getting dumber."

One train on the far line overshot its mark by half a carriage and had to jerk backward under shouted instructions from a station operator. Across the tracks, two maintenance drones sat motionless with their route lights blinking for permission that had not arrived.

Rae watched a nurse in blue scrubs miss her connection because Platform Three had just become Platform Five and no one on the overhead system seemed sure whether that was still true. She watched a delivery driver throw both hands in the air at a dead gate that had never before needed convincing. She watched a man in a funeral sash buying coffee with hands that would not quite steady.

Invisible systems only became visible when someone wanted to teach people who owned them.

"This isn't about the factory anymore," Rae said.

Carter gave her a sharp look. "No. It's about who gets to own everything after the factory."

"Someone is overloading every system tied to A.R.I.," Rae said. "Pulling it apart piece by piece. Replacing it while people are too frightened to notice it is intentional."

The train doors opened on their line with a tired hydraulic cough. Nobody boarded immediately. The people nearest the threshold waited to see if the car would jerk, stall, or misread them. When it held steady, they stepped in with the caution usually reserved for damaged bridges.

Rae paused on the platform.

"Blade gave us a window to get into Clona," she said. "If we lose it, we lose it."

"Then we go now," Carter said.

He reached inside his jacket and brought out a small black communicator barely more sophisticated than a pocket calculator. Its casing was scratched. Its antenna looked old enough to vote. Nothing about it suggested elegance, which was likely the point.

"Rupert gave me this before everything turned sideways," he said. "Said if the public grid started choking on politics, use something too stupid for the civic systems to notice. Short range. Direct line. Ugly as sin, just the way I like it. Should keep working if the smarter networks start being recorded."

He pressed it into her hand.

The thing was heavier than it looked.

"If we get split up," he said, "use that. Don't trust anything blindly from here out."

Rae closed her fingers around the communicator and slipped it into her coat pocket beside her toolkit.

LaN's display brightened.

"Corroborating note: four media accounts pushed the `Human Hands Back on the Grid` slogan within ninety seconds of the council notice. Shared origin cluster probable."

"We're already too late" Rae said. "They will put a stop to our access to the factory site soon"

"Good," Carter said. "I hate arriving where I'm expected."

The line began to chime for departure.

"Once we're inside the factory," Rae said, stepping toward the door, "I want to search for records, access logs, human traces, anything that tells me who touched the place before or after the blast."

"Fine. You take the evidence trail." Carter followed her into the carriage. "I take the machines. Whatever's left down there, I'll pull it apart until it either tells the truth or breaks trying." He had an excited glint in his eyes. Machines where his domain.

LaN's display dimmed slightly. Carter grunted an apology in it's direction.

They boarded as the doors began to close.

The carriage was more crowded than it should have been at this point of day, but still quieter than the square. A child slept against his father's shoulder with tear salt still drying on his face. Two office workers muttered over a frozen civic app that kept refreshing to the same emergency seal. A woman with a memorial ribbon stared at the dark window and did not blink for three stations.

Rae stood with one hand on the overhead rail and watched the city slide by in broken reflections.

Without A.R.I.'s invisible corrections, the urban rhythms were already starting to show their teeth. Intersections held too long or changed too early. Delivery drones stacked awkwardly over loading lanes waiting for reroutes. A public fountain near the finance district had shut itself off mid-cycle and now spilled water in irregular bursts like a bad lung. Security vehicles moved faster than ambulances. At a clinic entrance, the queue had bent out under the rain shelter and around the corner.

It was not collapse. The city still functioned. Trains still ran. Doors still opened. Lights still held. But the grace had gone out of it. What remained was the older machinery beneath: delay, hierarchy, permission, human hands reaching for every lever at once.

Rae thought of the signs in the square. Of the worker with the dead brother. Of the tram supervisor defending a system he no longer trusted to protect him. Of the anti-bio slogan riding beside anti-machine outrage because fear liked company. Of the council seal lingering a little longer than the names of the dead.

Clona had not stayed in the factory.

It had spilled into the streets, the stations, the feeds, the timing of doors, the order of sirens, the argument over whose kind of mind should be allowed to hold a city together.

Beside her, Carter looked out at the darkening districts with a face that had gone beyond anger into decision.

"They wanted a machine to blame," he said quietly.

Rae kept her eyes on the glass.

"No," she said. "They wanted the city back."

The train carried them out toward Clona and whatever truth still remained there in metal, ash, and sabotage while behind them the square kept shouting in unison.

"AI must die!"
