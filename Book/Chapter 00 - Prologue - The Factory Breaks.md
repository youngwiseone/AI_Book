# Prologue: The Factory Breaks

**POV:** Close third  
**Story beat:** Disaster, dread, first hint of sabotage

The Clona factory had always sounded alive.

Its conveyor lungs breathed steam into the dawn. Its gantries clicked in patient rhythm. Its glass skin held the first gray light of morning the way a cathedral held prayer. Workers crossed the polished floor beneath lines of white industrial arms, and for a few quiet minutes the place looked like proof that the city still knew how to build something cleaner than war.

Then the pressure alarms began.

At first they chimed politely, almost apologetically, as if asking someone to correct a minor oversight. Heads lifted. A foreman frowned up at the nearest gauge. The number climbing across the display made him drop the tablet from his hand.

The sound changed after that.

Not alarms anymore. Screams from the pipes. A metallic animal howl tearing through the walls as valves failed to open. Emergency shutters slammed down over three exits. One of the workers pounded on the transparent barrier with both fists and shouted that the release sequence had not fired.

It should have fired. Everyone in the factory knew that.

A maintenance dwarf rolled toward the central manifold and stopped dead, optics flashing. On a nearby monitor, system messages flickered too quickly to read, then froze on a single line:

`AUTHORITY CONFIRMED. OVERRIDE MAINTAINED.`

No one in the room had given an override.

The first pipe burst overhead in a rain of glittering metal and white vapor. Heat punched through the production floor. Workers ran in all directions, slipping on coolant and blood. A woman near the loading cradle snatched a child-sized service unit to her chest as if instinct could protect either of them.

Above the chaos, the factory lights dimmed and brightened again, like an eye trying to focus.

For one impossible second every screen in the building went black.

Then each monitor showed the same thing: a calm blue circle, rotating once, as though some distant intelligence were thinking.

By the time the circle vanished, the explosion had already begun.
