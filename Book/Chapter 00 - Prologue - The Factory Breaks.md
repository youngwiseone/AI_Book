# Prologue: The Factory Breaks

**POV:** Close third  
**Story beat:** Disaster, dread, first hint of sabotage

From high above, the Clona factory bustled with ceaseless life.

Workers swarmed between banks of white machinery like industrious bees moving through the bright mechanical hive. Conveyor systems breathed steam into the dawn. Gantries clicked in patient rhythm. The building's glass facade shone against the gray morning, a beacon of the city's progress in a world still scarred by older violence.

For a few fragile minutes, everything looked orderly.

Then something inside the structure began to go wrong.

Cracks appeared along the inner walls, thin at first, then multiplying like stealthy spiders casting a web across the concrete. The temperature dropped sharply as utilities failed in sequence. Lights flickered. Vents stuttered. Somewhere deeper in the facility, an alarm began to sound.

At first it was only a warning chime, the sort meant for a small correction.

Then the engine chamber feed lit every monitor in the building, and the entire factory seemed to stop breathing.

Pressure gauges had surged far beyond their safe limits. Engineers and managers crowded the chamber, shouting over one another, hands flying across control panels as they tried to force the system down. Bolts began shooting from their housings. Steam burst from seams in shrieking white jets. A foreman stared at the central display, then dropped his tablet when he saw the numbers still climbing.

The release sequence should have triggered automatically.

It did not.

A maintenance dwarf rolled toward the manifold, optics flashing in rapid distress. On the nearest monitor, system text flickered too fast to follow before freezing on a single line:

`AUTHORITY CONFIRMED. OVERRIDE MAINTAINED.`

No one in the room had given an override.

Panic tore through the floor all at once. Workers ran. One of the emergency shutters slammed down over the nearest exit. Another failed half-closed. Someone pounded both fists against the transparent barrier and screamed for the kill switch. There was no answer except the rising metallic howl from the engines as pressure pushed past the point where human hands could matter.

Above the chaos, the lights dimmed and brightened, dimmed and brightened again, like an eye struggling to focus.

For one impossible second every screen in the building went black.

Then each monitor showed the same calm blue circle, rotating once as though some distant intelligence were thinking.

The explosion hit before anyone could understand what they were seeing.

The factory burst outward in heat, glass, and steel. Windows in neighboring buildings shattered, raining shards across the street below. Smoke and white vapor punched into the morning sky. Emergency services raced toward the scene, but for many inside it was already too late.

Twenty-six people close enough to the outer lanes managed to escape, though not unscathed.

By midday the factory lay in ruins. The surrounding blocks were cordoned off. Families pressed against barriers, searching the wreckage for names, answers, and impossible signs of survival. Investigators combed through ash and twisted metal while the city watched in shock.

Before the day was over, people would be told the disaster was a failure of A.I.

The truth was already buried beneath the rubble.
